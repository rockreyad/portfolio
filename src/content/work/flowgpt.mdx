<CaseSection eyebrow="Context" title="Joined FlowGPT in the founding phase.">

FlowGPT — now part of Kaon — is an open-source AI app creator and prompt-sharing platform. [TechCrunch described it](https://techcrunch.com/2024/02/26/flowgpt-is-the-wild-west-of-genai-apps/) as a _"sort of app store for generative AI models like Google's Gemini, Anthropic's Claude, Meta's Llama 2 and OpenAI's DALL-E 3, as well as front-end experiences for those models."_ It was co-founded by Jay Dang (CEO, UC Berkeley CS dropout) and Lifan Wang (former engineering manager at Amazon) in 2023, headquartered in Berkeley.

I joined in **June 2023**, in the founding phase, while the team was still small enough to fit in one room. The product hadn't yet hit its inflection point. ChatGPT and prompt engineering were just becoming a mainstream conversation, and FlowGPT was about to ride that wave harder than almost anyone else.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="The wave" title="From ~1M to 6M users in three months.">

In my first three months on the team, FlowGPT scaled from approximately **one million to six million users**. That growth wasn't linear — it was the curve you get when a product hits a cultural moment correctly. ChatGPT was the rocket; FlowGPT became the marketplace people used alongside it once they realized "good prompts" was a real category.

During the peak overlap, internal tracking showed FlowGPT capturing roughly **27% of ChatGPT visitor traffic** — i.e., a meaningful fraction of people leaving or supplementing ChatGPT were ending up on us. That's the single most important number from this period: it's not "we got users," it's "we became part of the loop people were already running."

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="Role" title="What full-stack actually meant on this team.">

Engineering at FlowGPT during that growth phase wasn't _one feature, ship, repeat_. It was: pick the highest-leverage thing this week, design it end-to-end, ship it, instrument it, and move on. Day-to-day that meant working across:

- **Marketplace surfaces** — prompt detail, search, discovery — that had to render fast under SEO load and hold up while the user table was 6× larger than the month before
- **Authenticated chat surfaces** — streaming, optimistic updates, retry behaviour, the kind of latency-sensitive flow where every shortcut becomes an incident later
- **Backend plumbing on a T3 stack** — Next.js + tRPC + Prisma against Postgres, with Redis for caching and BullMQ for background jobs (recommendations, indexing, embeddings, the heavy operations you don't want in the request path)
- **Reviewing PRs and pushing for changes that hold up under load** — at our growth rate, "works on my machine" had a half-life of about 48 hours

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="Stack" title="What we ran on, and why it earned its place.">

- **Next.js + tRPC + Prisma** — the T3 stack core. End-to-end type safety reduces production incidents more than almost any other engineering investment at startup scale.
- **Postgres** as source of truth, **Redis** for hot-path caching and rate limits, **BullMQ** for background work that doesn't belong in the request cycle.
- **TypeScript everywhere**, strict mode, no `any`-bailouts in code review.
- **Tailwind** for UI, with a discipline-based component layer rather than a third-party design system — the team chose ergonomics over ceremony.

The lesson I carry forward: stack choice matters a fraction as much as the discipline you apply to it. The T3 stack at FlowGPT shipped well because we treated session validation, idempotency, and error boundaries as features, not fixtures.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="What I'd defend in a senior interview" title="Three things I learned that I still apply.">

- **Growth phases don&apos;t reward cleverness; they punish slop.** When the user table is doubling every six weeks, every shortcut you took yesterday becomes a 2am page tomorrow. I write more defensively than most engineers because of FlowGPT.
- **The right architecture decision is the one you can defend in code review three months later.** Moving prompt detail from CSR to RSC + ISR, choosing Postgres full-text over a managed search service, picking BullMQ over a roll-your-own queue — none of these were the _smartest_ choice in isolation. They were the choices the team could maintain.
- **A small founding team is a different organism than a 150-person company, and you have to learn both.** I joined a startup; I left a structured engineering org. The transition is one of the most useful things I&apos;ve learned that doesn&apos;t appear on any tech-stack list.

</CaseSection>
