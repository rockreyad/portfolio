<CaseSection eyebrow="Context" title="An Oxford-based AI startup, with founders who had real CVs.">

[TakeNote.ai](https://www.takenote.ai) is an Oxford, UK-based AI meeting-intelligence startup. It was co-founded by [Robin Paine](https://www.linkedin.com/in/rpaine/) — former CTO of the **London Stock Exchange** and the **London Metal Exchange**, with a career spanning M&amp;A, programme management, and large-scale technology delivery — and [Trung Thanh Tran](https://www.linkedin.com/in/trungthanhtran/), an AI researcher and Data Scientist at Pixta Vietnam, working in foundation models, computer vision, and LLMs.

I joined as a **founding engineer on the web side**, working remotely from Bangladesh alongside [Md. Maruf Ahmed](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mrmarufpro/) — a Software Engineer with a Shopify-app and React/Node.js background. The four of us were the team: two co-founders, two engineers.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="The bet" title="Empty repo to launched product.">

The brief was straightforward in scope, hard in execution: take the founders&apos; vision for an AI-powered meeting intelligence product and turn it into something a customer could log into and use. There was no codebase. No schema. No deployed surface. There was a vision, two co-founders, and the two of us.

I owned the parts of the stack closest to the user:

- The **web app shell** — routing, layout, theming, state ownership
- **Auth and the session boundary** — including the security and data-residency thinking that an FCA-adjacent product needs from day one
- The **note-taking surface** itself — editor, persistence, sync behaviour
- **Build and deploy plumbing** for the web side

Maruf and I split the engineering load; the co-founders held vision, AI architecture, and customer development. In a four-person company, scope isn&apos;t assigned — it&apos;s chosen. _"Founding engineer"_ meant exactly what the title implied: there was no one to escalate to.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="What I shipped" title="A v1 product. Live, end-to-end.">

We launched. The product worked. Users could sign up, run meetings, get transcripts and summaries — the full loop the founders had specced.

What we _didn&apos;t_ hit was first revenue before my departure, and the company didn&apos;t raise its next round on the original product positioning. I&apos;m saying that plainly because the alternative — pretending the v1 was a commercial success — wouldn&apos;t survive a single recruiter call. What we shipped was a working product. What didn&apos;t happen was a market.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="Why I departed" title="The reality of remote founding-engineer work.">

I left before the next chapter, for reasons I&apos;ll lay out plainly because they&apos;re instructive:

- **The company wanted in-person operation.** The founders are in Oxford; I was in Bangladesh. As the product matured the team needed tighter, in-room iteration. Remote founding-engineer work is a real model, but it has limits for an early-stage company.
- **Equity-related circumstances.** I joined an equity-funded UK company on a remote arrangement. As the situation evolved, the equity arrangement and operating model didn&apos;t converge in a way that worked for either side.
- **The next round didn&apos;t come together.** The team didn&apos;t close additional funding on the original product positioning, and the project went into a transition period.

I made the call to step away on good terms before the situation forced one.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="What it became" title="The product I built kept going — into a different market.">

[The site at takenote.ai](https://www.takenote.ai) is live today, but the product has **pivoted**. It&apos;s now positioned specifically for **FCA-regulated financial advisers** — the UK financial-services compliance niche. The current version touts a 3% Word Error Rate, MiFID II 5-year retention, FCA suitability note structure, and UK data residency on Azure UK South.

That pivot isn&apos;t mine to claim. But the foundation we shipped in v1 is what the team had to build on top of. The product I helped take from an empty repo became the chassis for a more focused, more defensible market positioning. That&apos;s as good an outcome as a founding engineer who stepped away early can hope for.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection eyebrow="What I'd defend in a senior interview" title="Three lessons from being a remote founding engineer.">

- **A founding engineer&apos;s job is to ship the v1, not to guarantee the market.** I owned the technical execution — empty repo to launched product. I didn&apos;t own customer development, fundraising, or pivot strategy. Being clear about which lever you&apos;re pulling is the senior version of "I&apos;m a founding engineer." Anyone who claims they own all of it on a four-person team is selling something.
- **Remote founding-engineer arrangements need more contractual care than they get.** Equity, scope, geography, and decision rights all have to be aligned in writing before the first commit, not after the first product. I learned this the harder way at TakeNote, and it&apos;s shaped how I&apos;d sign onto an early-stage equity arrangement now.
- **Stepping away on good terms is a senior skill.** When the operating model and the company&apos;s needs diverge, the engineer who exits cleanly — code documented, knowledge transferred, no scorched earth — leaves both parties in a better place than the one who hangs on. The TakeNote team kept building after I left. That&apos;s the win condition.

</CaseSection>
